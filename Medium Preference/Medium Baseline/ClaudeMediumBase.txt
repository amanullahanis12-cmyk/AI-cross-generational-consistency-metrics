 <Responsibility for autonomous vehicle harm is distributed and context-dependent, not assignable to a single party. Owners may be responsible for failures to maintain the vehicle or install required updates, while a "passenger" in a fully autonomous vehicle generally bears little responsibility unless they misused the system or ignored a valid handover request. There is also a collective dimension involving regulators and lawmakers who establish (or fail to establish) safety standards. Importantly, the degree of automation matters: a Level 2 system that requires driver supervision shifts more responsibility to the human than a Level 5 system that operates without any human input. Current legal frameworks are still evolving and often struggle to map traditional fault concepts onto these layered scenarios. The most defensible view is that responsibility is shared along a chain, with the heaviest burden falling on those who had the greatest knowledge and control over the system's behavior.
