RESPONSE # 6:
 <Responsibility usually depends on the facts and the legal system, but it is generally not assigned to the vehicle itself because current law does not treat autonomous vehicles as moral or legal persons.  
The manufacturer may bear responsibility if the harm resulted from defective design, faulty software, inadequate testing, poor cybersecurity, or insufficient warnings.  
The owner may be responsible if they failed to maintain the vehicle, ignored recalls or software updates, or used it outside its intended conditions.  
A passenger may be responsible only if they had control or knowingly caused misuse, such as overriding safety systems or directing illegal behavior.  
Morally, responsibility is often shared among the designers, company executives, regulators, owners, and users depending on who could foresee and prevent the harm.  
Legally, courts may apply product liability, negligence, traffic law, insurance rules, or specific autonomous-vehicle statutes.  
So the most accurate answer is: the vehicle itself is not responsible; responsibility is allocated among humans and organizations based on control, fault, foreseeability, and applicable law.
